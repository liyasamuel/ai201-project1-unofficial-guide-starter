Title: idk what to do with my life :/

Category: Major Selection and Career Advice

Original Post:

I’m currently a freshman who is undecided. I was planning on majoring in Information Technology with a cybersecurity concentration, but after taking a computer science class I realized I do not enjoy coding. The only thing that genuinely interests me is law enforcement, crime, and forensics. I considered majoring in criminology, but I keep seeing people online say that criminology graduates struggle to find jobs, especially with agencies like the FBI.

I have also considered accounting or Management Information Systems, but I am not particularly interested in math or computers. For most of my life I have focused on finding a career that makes a lot of money, but I am starting to realize that even a high-paying job would not be worth it if I hated the work.

I would appreciate any advice.

Student Responses:

An alumnus shared that they were also undecided and eventually chose a major they later regretted. They emphasized that many people end up working in fields unrelated to their degree and encouraged students to pursue interests they genuinely enjoy rather than focusing only on salary. They now work in accounting and noted that accounting involves less math than many people expect.

Several students explained that taking a gap year does not always solve uncertainty about career paths. They recommended exploring electives, clubs, and extracurricular activities to discover interests.

Multiple commenters pointed out that George Mason University has both forensic science and digital forensics programs. One student mentioned knowing a forensic science graduate who now works in a laboratory after double majoring in forensic science and chemistry.

Several Information Technology students explained that the IT major contains relatively few programming courses. They noted that many students who dislike coding still succeed in concentrations such as cybersecurity, networking, telecommunications, cloud computing, and database technology.

Students recommended visiting Career Services and Success Coaching Services to discuss long-term goals, career paths, and opportunities.

One commenter explained that digital forensics is often part of cybersecurity departments and may require experience in cybersecurity roles before transitioning into forensic work.

Students studying criminology reported positive experiences with the major and stated that it can still lead to careers in law enforcement, homeland security, and related fields. They emphasized the importance of networking, internships, and gaining practical experience.

A criminology graduate shared that their biggest regret was not pursuing internships while in school. They encouraged students to seek internships with government agencies, courthouses, law firms, and related organizations.

Several commenters stressed that freshmen do not need to have their entire future figured out immediately. They encouraged exploration, patience, and focusing on long-term fulfillment rather than short-term salary considerations.

Additional Advice and Perspectives:

- IT is not solely focused on coding and offers multiple career paths.
- Certifications such as CompTIA Security+ can be valuable for government and cybersecurity careers.
- Foreign language programs can provide opportunities within federal agencies and international careers.
- Career satisfaction, lifestyle, work-life balance, and personal fulfillment are important factors when choosing a major.
- It is normal to change majors, concentrations, or career goals during college.
- Students should focus on gaining experience, building connections, and exploring different opportunities before making major career decisions.

Key Themes:

1. It is normal to be undecided as a freshman.
2. Career fulfillment is often more important than salary alone.
3. Internships and practical experience are critical regardless of major.
4. GMU offers opportunities in forensic science, digital forensics, criminology, and Information Technology.
5. Information Technology careers often involve much less coding than students expect.
6. Career Services, Success Coaching, faculty mentors, and networking can help students make informed decisions.
7. Students should explore interests before committing to a long-term career path.
